About Us

The study explores the **effects of rainfall on groundwater levels**, incorporating factors like population usage and climatic conditions to predict future groundwater availability. Groundwater is a critical resource for domestic, agricultural, and industrial uses, especially in regions with limited surface water supply. However, excessive extraction, changing rainfall patterns, and increasing population demand pose significant challenges to its sustainability. Using **machine learning algorithms**—Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Linear Regression—this analysis models the complex interplay between rainfall, population usage, and climatic conditions to forecast the **Net Ground Water Availability for future use**. These algorithms are chosen for their ability to capture non-linear relationships (Random Forest and Gradient Boosting) and provide baseline comparisons (Linear Regression). Evaluation metrics like R², MSE, MAE, RMSE, and MAPE assess the model's accuracy. Explainable AI techniques, including SHAP and LIME, enhance interpretability by identifying key drivers of groundwater availability, such as rainfall recharge, current extraction levels, and seasonal variations. The findings aim to support sustainable groundwater management by identifying high-risk regions and enabling policymakers to devise strategies for water conservation. This data-driven approach ensures a robust prediction framework, fostering resilience against climate variability and population growth pressures on groundwater resources.
